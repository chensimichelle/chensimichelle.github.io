Rate Determination Practical

CIE A-Level Chemistry 9701 · Thiosulfate Clock Reaction

1 The Chemistry

S₂O₃²⁻(aq) + 2H⁺(aq) → S(s) + SO₂(g) + H₂O(l)
  • Thiosulfate ions decompose in acid to form a fine suspension of sulfur (S).
  • The sulfur makes the solution cloudy — used to time the reaction.
  • Reaction is stopped (timed) when the cross/print beneath the beaker is JUST no longer visible.

2 Key Principles

2.1   Why use 1/t as a measure of rate?

  • A faster reaction produces the fixed amount of sulfur in less time.
  • So:  rate ∝ 1/t  (the shorter the time, the faster the rate).

2.2   Why use volume as a measure of concentration?

  • Concentration ∝ volume of solution used, PROVIDED the total volume is kept constant in every experiment.
  • This is why you add water to make up the total volume to 55 cm³ in each experiment.
⚠️ Critical Rule
  • TOTAL VOLUME must be exactly the same in every experiment (e.g. 55 cm³).
  • Only ONE variable (e.g. volume of FA1) should change at a time.
  • All other volumes (acid, water top-up) must be adjusted accordingly.

2.3   Why plot log(1/t) vs log(V)?

  • The rate equation is: rate = k[S₂O₃²⁻]ⁿ, which gives: 1/t = k·Vⁿ
  • Taking logs of both sides: log(1/t) = n·log(V) + log(k)
  • This is y = mx + c — a straight line where the GRADIENT = n = order of reaction.
  • Plotting 1/t vs V directly gives a curve, which is harder to interpret.
Plot Graph shape Can find order?
1/t  vs  V Curve ❌ Not easily
log(1/t)  vs  log(V) Straight line ✅ Gradient = order

3 Setting Up Your Results Table

Your table MUST include these columns:
  • Volume of FA 1 / cm³
  • Volume of water / cm³  (to keep total constant)
  • Time / s  (recorded to nearest second)
  • 1/t  or  log(1/t)
  • log(volume of FA 1)  (if required by question)
⚠️ Common Table Mistakes to Avoid
  • Missing units in column headings — every column needs a unit.
  • Forgetting to include Experiments 1 & 2 in the same table.
  • Not drawing the table in advance (before doing experiments).
  • Using T instead of t for time.
  • Not recording times to the nearest second.
  • Total volume is not constant across experiments.

✅ Correct heading format examples:

  • Volume of FA 1 / cm³   OR   Volume of FA 1 (cm³)
  • Time / s   OR   t / s
  • 1/t / s⁻¹   OR   log(1/t)

4 Choosing Your Concentrations

  • You need a MINIMUM of 5 experiments total (Expts 1 & 2 + at least 3 additional).
  • Volumes of FA 1 should be spread evenly across the full range (e.g. 10, 20, 30, 40, 50 cm³).
  • More points = more reliable graph and more tolerance for anomalous results.
⚠️ Why spread your volumes evenly?
  • Bunched points (e.g. 40, 42, 44, 46, 50) give a graph covering less than 20×20 small squares — you LOSE the straight line mark.
  • Evenly spaced volumes give a line that spans the full graph area, making the gradient easier to calculate accurately.

5 Checking Accuracy — The Vt Test

The examiner checks your accuracy using the Vt product:

The Vt Constant
  • If the reaction is first order, then: concentration × time = constant
  • Since volume ∝ concentration: V × t should be approximately constant across experiments.
  • Examiners calculate Vt for Experiment 1 and your two largest-volume additional experiments.
Vt difference Marks awarded
Both values within 10% of the larger of the closest pair 2 marks (full)
Difference between 10% and 20% 1 mark (partial)
Difference greater than 20% 0 marks
Example check:
  • V=50, t=35 → Vt = 1750
  • V=40, t=55 → Vt = 2200
  • V=30, t=69 → Vt = 2070
  • Closest pair: 2200 and 2070. Difference = 130. 10% of 2200 = 220. 130 < 220 ✅ → Full marks.

6 Plotting the Graph

  • Plot log(1/t) on the y-axis and log(volume of FA 1) on the x-axis.
  • Choose scales so that points cover MORE than half of each axis.
  • Plot ALL experiments (including Expts 1 & 2).
  • Draw a single best-fit straight line through the points.
⚠️ Graph Rules
  • Points must be plotted to within ½ small square.
  • If you only have 3 points, NONE can be anomalous.
  • If you have 4+ points, ONE anomalous point is acceptable.
  • The best-fit line must span at least 20×20 small squares — do not bunch your points.
  • Use construction lines covering at least half the line length to calculate the gradient.

7 Calculating the Gradient (Order of Reaction)

  • Draw a large triangle using construction lines on your graph.
  • The triangle hypotenuse should cover at least HALF the length of your line.
  • Read coordinates carefully (to nearest ½ small square).
  • Gradient = Δy / Δx = change in log(1/t) / change in log(V)
  • The gradient = order of reaction with respect to thiosulfate.
  • Give the answer to at least 1 decimal place.

8 Uncertainty & Percentage Error

The endpoint (when print just disappears) is a judgment call — it introduces uncertainty.

Experiment Why? Uncertainty
Expt 1 (fast reaction, large V) Change is rapid — easier to judge the exact moment Smaller (≤ 3 s)
Expt 2 (slow reaction, small V) Change is gradual — harder to judge, more variable Larger (≥ 3 s, up to 10 s)
  • Percentage uncertainty = (uncertainty in seconds / reaction time) × 100%
  • Faster reactions have SMALLER percentage uncertainty, even if the absolute uncertainty (seconds) is similar.

9 Investigating H⁺ Concentration (Part f)

  • Keep the volume of FA 1 CONSTANT (same thiosulfate concentration).
  • Change the volume of FA 2 (acid) — this changes [H⁺].
  • Add water to keep the TOTAL volume constant at 55 cm³.
⚠️ Remember
  • Only the volume of FA 2 (acid) should change.
  • Volume of FA 1 must stay the same as in Experiment 2.
  • Adjust water accordingly: water = 55 − V(FA1) − V(FA2).
Expected result: Little change in reaction time as acid concentration is doubled → rate is approximately zero order with respect to H⁺.

10 Quick Reference Checklist

  • Total volume is the same in every experiment
  • Table drawn up in advance with correct headings and units
  • Times recorded to nearest second (use t not T)
  • At least 3 extra experiments beyond Expts 1 & 2
  • Volumes of FA 1 spread evenly across the full range
  • log(1/t) plotted on y-axis, log(V) on x-axis
  • Points cover more than half of each axis
  • Best-fit straight line drawn
  • Construction lines cover at least half the line
  • Gradient calculated correctly to ≥ 1 d.p.
  • Uncertainty for Expt 2 > Uncertainty for Expt 1
  • For H⁺ investigation: only acid volume changes, FA 1 constant
